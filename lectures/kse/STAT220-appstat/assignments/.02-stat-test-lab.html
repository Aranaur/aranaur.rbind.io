1 Objective

In Lab 1 you designed a one-sample A/B experiment for your own business case and walked through the basic test mechanics: hypotheses, critical region, \(p\)-value, power, and MDE.

In Lab 2 you take the same business case and go beyond a single accept/reject decision. The focus of this lab is the confidence interval — the object that actually drives product decisions in practice, because it tells the stakeholder how much the metric could have moved, not just whether it moved.

You will:

  1. Wrap your test logic into a reusable my_binom_test() function that returns a full structured result (estimate, \(p\)-value, CI, decision).
  2. Build confidence intervals by several methods, compare them, and verify that they actually deliver the promised coverage.
  3. Run an A/A test to confirm that your testing infrastructure is correctly calibrated before any real launch.
  4. Combine all of the above into a single stakeholder recommendation.

3 Tasks

3.1 Recap of the Lab 1 Case

  1. In one paragraph, restate the business problem, the success metric, and the baseline \(\mu_0\).
  2. State the hypotheses, the chosen significance level \(\alpha\), and the business MDE (with its monetary justification from Lab 1).
  3. Briefly summarise what you found in Lab 1: the planned sample size \(N\), the observed \(q\), the \(p\)-value, and your decision.
  4. If you discovered a methodological issue in Lab 1 (wrong alternative, misjudged MDE, etc.), state the fix here and use the corrected numbers throughout Lab 2.

3.2 A Reusable my_binom_test() Function

  1. Implement a function my_binom_test(q, n, mu0, alternative, alpha, conf_int) with the following contract:
    • Supports alternative \(\in\) {"greater", "less", "two-sided"}. For the two-sided case use \(p = 2 \cdot \min(p_{\text{left}}, p_{\text{right}})\).
    • When conf_int=True, also returns a confidence interval built by inverting the test (the same algorithm shown in the lecture and Practice 2).
    • Returns a dict with at least: estimate, p_value, reject, ci_low, ci_high.
  2. Apply the function to your Lab 1 observation \(q\). Print the full result for the alternative your business actually cares about.
  3. Verify your implementation against scipy.stats.binomtest (this is the only legitimate use of the built-in function). Show that \(p\)-values match to at least 4 decimal places. If the CI differs slightly from binomtest.proportion_ci, explain why (which construction does each one use?).

3.3 Confidence Intervals: Construction

  1. For your observed \(q\) and \(n\), compute a \(95\%\) confidence interval using four different constructions:
    • Inversion (your own my_binom_test, two-sided).
    • Clopper–Pearson (exact) via proportion_confint(method="beta").
    • Wilson via proportion_confint(method="wilson").
    • Normal (Wald) approximation via proportion_confint(method="normal").
  2. Put all four intervals into a small table with columns method | lower | upper | width.
  3. Comment on the differences. In particular:
    • Which intervals are guaranteed to lie inside \([0, 1]\), and which can produce nonsense bounds for small \(n\) or extreme \(\hat{\mu}\)?
    • Why is the Wald interval almost always the narrowest, and why is that not a good reason to prefer it?
    • Which two methods coincide for moderate \(n\) and \(\hat{\mu}\) near \(0.5\)?
  4. One-sided lower bound. Using your my_binom_test with alternative set to the side your business cares about, compute the one-sided \(95\%\) bound. Verify the lecture equivalence: the one-sided lower bound at level \(1 - \alpha\) equals the lower edge of the two-sided interval at level \(1 - 2\alpha\). Show both numbers side by side.

3.4 Confidence Intervals: Properties and Interpretation

  1. Coverage simulation. Pick a “true” value latent_mu for your metric (you may use the value from Lab 1) and your sample size \(N\). Run \(\geq 5\,000\) independent simulated experiments under this latent_mu. For each one, build a \(95\%\) confidence interval using either your inversion-based interval or Wilson. Report the empirical coverage probability (the fraction of intervals that contain latent_mu). It should be \(\geq 0.95\). Why is the actual coverage usually slightly above \(0.95\) for the binomial?
  2. CI width vs. sample size. Holding \(\hat{\mu} = q/n\) constant at your Lab 1 value, plot the Wilson CI width as a function of \(N\) for \(N\) from a small value up to \(\geq 5N_{\text{plan}}\). On the plot, mark:
    • The Lab 1 planned \(N\).
    • The \(N\) at which the CI shrinks to a width your business considers “tight enough” — and explain that threshold in monetary terms (one paragraph).
  3. Decision rule from a CI. Suppose your business agreed on a threshold \(\mu^{*}\) above which the new variant gets shipped (e.g. \(\mu^{*} = \mu_0 + \text{business MDE}\)). Translate the following three outcomes into ship/iterate/extend decisions, and justify each:
    • Lower CI bound is \(\geq \mu^{*}\).
    • CI contains both \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu^{*}\).
    • Lower CI bound is between \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu^{*}\).
  4. Common pitfalls. In one short paragraph, state in plain English:
    • What the \(95\%\) confidence level does mean.
    • What it does not mean (the “probability of the parameter” trap). Why is this distinction more than pedantic — give a scenario in which the wrong interpretation would lead to a wrong decision.

3.5 BONUS: A/A Test: Validating Your Infrastructure

Before any real A/B test goes to production, the standard sanity check is an A/A test: split users into two groups that get the same experience and confirm that the test rejects \(H_0\) no more often than \(\alpha\). If it does, the testing platform itself is broken (bad randomisation, leaked events, sample-ratio mismatch, …) and any subsequent A/B result is meaningless.

  1. Run \(\geq 5\,000\) simulated A/A experiments under \(H_0\) with your sample size \(N\) and your baseline \(\mu_0\). For each one, compute the \(p\)-value using your my_binom_test.
  2. Plot the histogram of \(p\)-values. Comment on its shape:
    • Why is it expected to be approximately uniform on \([0, 1]\)?
    • Why does the binomial give a slightly “stepped” pattern, and what would a broken infrastructure histogram look like (sketch the shape verbally)?
  3. Report the empirical FPR (fraction of \(p \leq \alpha\)). Compute a \(95\%\) Wilson CI around this empirical FPR. Is the nominal \(\alpha\) inside it? Use this to argue, with simulation evidence, that your test is correctly calibrated.
  4. Two product-specific failure modes. Describe two realistic ways a real A/A test could fail for your particular product (not generic “data is wrong”). Examples of the right level of specificity: “the same household IP appears in both groups”, “the tutorial event is logged once for re-installers and counted twice”, “weekend traffic is exclusively routed to group A”. For each failure mode, predict what the \(p\)-value histogram would look like.

3.6 Final Recommendation

Putting all of the above together, write a single-page recommendation memo (≤ 400 words) addressed to a non-technical stakeholder. It must contain:

  1. The observed point estimate, the chosen \(95\%\) confidence interval (state which construction and why), and the one-sided lower bound.
  2. The decision (ship / iterate / extend the test) and the reason behind it, framed in terms of the CI and the business threshold \(\mu^{*}\) — not just the \(p\)-value.
  3. One honest caveat: did the A/A check pass cleanly, or are there open questions about the infrastructure?

The memo must be readable by someone who has never heard the word “p-value”. You will be graded on whether a real product manager could act on it.

TipGrading Details

Correctness of methodology (3 pts):

  • my_binom_test implements all three alternatives correctly and matches scipy.stats.binomtest on the \(p\)-value (1 pt).
  • All four CI constructions are computed correctly, and the one-sided / two-sided equivalence (level \(1-\alpha\) vs. \(1-2\alpha\)) is verified numerically (1 pt).
  • The coverage simulation is set up correctly (independent samples, correct \(\geq 0.95\) result, conservative-coverage explanation) (1 pt).
  • A/A simulation produces an empirical FPR that is statistically indistinguishable from \(\alpha\), with a Wilson CI to back it up (1 pt).

Interpretation of results (3 pts):

  • The four-method CI comparison shows real understanding (not a copy of textbook descriptions) — you explain why Wald is dangerous and why Clopper–Pearson is conservative.
  • The decision rule based on \(\mu^{*}\) is concrete and tied to the business case.
  • The “what a CI does not mean” paragraph contains a real scenario, not just a slogan.
  • A/A failure modes are realistic and product-specific.

Code quality and organization (2 pts):

  • my_binom_test is a single reusable function used across all subsequent sections (not re-implemented inline).
  • Plots have axis labels, titles, and legible legends.
  • Narrative text precedes every code block.

Defense

During the defense (5–7 minutes) you will be asked “What if?” questions that probe whether you understand why each construction exists, for example:

  • “Your Wilson CI is narrower than your inversion-based CI. Which one should you put in the dashboard, and why?”
  • “The PM says: ‘I see a 95% CI of [0.61, 0.69]. Doesn’t that mean there is a 95% chance the true rate is in that range?’ What is your one-sentence answer?”
  • “Your coverage simulation gave 0.962 instead of 0.95. Is that a bug, or expected? How would you tell the difference?”
  • “Your A/A test gave an empirical FPR of 0.07 instead of 0.05. Is the difference statistically meaningful, or is it just simulation noise? Show me the calculation.”
  • “Switch your test from one-sided to two-sided in your code right now. By how much does the lower CI bound move, and does that change your recommendation?”
  • “Set \(N\) to half of your Lab 1 plan and re-run the CI. What does the new width tell you about whether the test is actually decisive?”

You may also be asked to change one parameter live in your code and explain the resulting shift.
