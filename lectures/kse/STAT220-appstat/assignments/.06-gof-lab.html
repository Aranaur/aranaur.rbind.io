1 Objective

In Lab 4 you ran a two-sample \(t\)-test on ARPU and recovered a business decision. In Lab 5 you went one level up and audited the criterion itself with Monte Carlo. Lab 6 is forensics: a clean \(t\)-test verdict is not enough, because the \(t\)-test only ever looks at the mean.

The GreenBowl experimentation platform shipped you the results of a new-menu A/B test, and the automated report shows a tidy \(t\)-test “ship / don’t ship” line. Your job is to decide whether you can believe it. The lecture gave you a toolkit where everything reduces to one idea — compare cumulative distribution functions — and several of those tools see things the mean cannot:

  • Shapiro–Wilk + QQ-plot — is the metric even normal enough to trust the \(t\)-test on a small sample?
  • Two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov — was the randomization honest (the A/A guardrail on the pre-period), and did the treatment change the distribution where the mean stayed flat?
  • Empirical CDF — where and how do the two groups differ?
  • Monte Carlo — is your own guardrail actually calibrated on skewed money data?

The twist, as in Labs 4–5: you do not get to see what is wrong. Your experiment is pulled live from greenbowl_gof.py, keyed to your student id. Some students received an honest experiment with a real mean lift; some received one where the effect is hidden in the shape; some received a broken randomization, a wave of churned users, a metric that must be analysed on the log scale, or a paired design masquerading as two independent groups. The only honest way to find out which one you hold is to run the diagnostics.

You will:

  1. Pull your experiment and diagnose the metric’s distribution (Shapiro + QQ), deciding whether to work on the raw or the log scale.
  2. Build a small, reusable dist_guardrail toolkit step by step and run the A/A guardrail (two-sample KS on the pre-period) to test whether the randomization is honest.
  3. Ask whether the effect lives in the shape, not the mean — comparing the \(t\)-test against KS + the ECDF on the experiment period.
  4. Run the independence check and, if your data are paired, redo the analysis the right way (per-user differences).
  5. Validate your guardrail with Monte Carlo — its FPR, the minimum \(n\), and its power on your kind of skewed data.
  6. Deliver a one-page forensic verdict that a non-statistician PM can act on.

3.2 Is the Metric Normal? (check_normality)

On a small sample the \(t\)-test relies on the normality of the data (a large \(n\) is rescued by the CLT). Before trusting any mean-based verdict, the metric must be checked — and a QQ-plot shows how it deviates, which a single \(p\)-value hides.

  1. Implement Version 1 of your toolkit, check_normality(sample, alpha=0.05), returning the Shapiro–Wilk \(W\), its \(p\)-value, and a human-readable verdict (mirror Practice 6).
  2. Run check_normality and a QQ-plot (scipy.stats.probplot) on your control arm, on the raw scale and on the log scale (np.log). Read the QQ-plots: where do the points leave the line?
  3. Mind the sample size. If your \(n\) is large (hundreds+), do not chase the Shapiro \(p\)-value — explain, in one sentence, why a normality test rejects “everything” at large \(n\) and why you judge normality by QQ + the CLT here. If your \(n\) is small (a few dozen), the test is meaningful — say what it tells you.
  4. Decide the scale. Based on 2–3, state whether you will analyse this metric on the raw or the log scale, and what changes about the meaning of “the effect” if you log it (arithmetic vs. geometric mean). If your sample is small, do not stop at the QQ-plot — a non-normal shape does not by itself break the \(t\)-test (a balanced two-sample test is surprisingly robust to skew). Treat the scale choice as a hypothesis and confirm it in Section 3.6 by checking whether the raw vs. log test is actually calibrated at your \(n\) and your arm balance.
TipResearch hook

The log transform is the analyst’s standard move for skewed money/time metrics. If check_normality flips from “reject” on the raw scale to “do not reject” on the log scale, that is a finding — but the QQ-plot alone does not prove the raw test is broken. Skew bites hardest on a small and unbalanced sample; on a balanced one the \(t\)-test largely shrugs it off. Earn the log-scale verdict with the calibration check in Section 3.6, don’t assume it.

3.3 The A/A Guardrail: Was the Randomization Honest? (same_distribution)

Before believing any effect, check that the split itself was clean: an honest randomization makes control and test share the whole distribution, not just the mean. This is a homogeneity question — the two-sample KS — and the right place to ask it is the pre-period, where no treatment effect can exist yet.

  1. Implement Version 2, same_distribution(x, y, alpha=0.05), using scipy.stats.ks_2samp, returning \(D\), the \(p\)-value, and a verdict (mirror Practice 6).
  2. Run it on the pre-period: same_distribution(exp.pre_control, exp.pre_test). Plot the two empirical CDFs on one axis.
  3. Interpret as a guardrail. A rejection on the pre-period is an alarm — no effect can exist there, so it means the groups were not comparable to begin with (a splitter / SRM-by-shape bug). State clearly: does your pre-period A/A pass or fail, and what that means for whether the experiment result can be trusted at all.
ImportantStop-the-line rule

If the pre-period A/A fails, the experiment-period comparison is meaningless until the randomization is fixed — a difference you see later cannot be attributed to the treatment. If it fails, say so plainly: the rest of your analysis becomes “here is the bug”, not “here is the effect”.

3.4 Does the Effect Live in the Shape, Not the Mean?

The lecture’s central thesis: the \(t\)-test answers only “did the mean move?”. An effect in variance, tails, or the share of zeros is invisible to it but caught by KS + the ECDF. (Run this section only if your pre-period A/A from Section 3.3 passed — otherwise note that a broken split makes the experiment comparison void, and treat the differences below as evidence of the bug, not of an effect.)

  1. On the experiment period, run both criteria on the same arms:
    • the \(t\)-test on the mean (scipy.stats.ttest_ind(..., equal_var=False)), and
    • same_distribution(exp.control, exp.test) (KS on the whole CDF). Tabulate the two \(p\)-values.
  2. Plot the two empirical CDFs of control vs. test. Annotate where they differ (a location shift? a fan-out at the tail? a jump at zero?).
  3. Diagnose the four possible patterns and say which one you are in, with the evidence:
    • \(t\) rejects and KS rejects → a genuine shift that also moves the mean (a clean win/loss);
    • \(t\) silent but KS rejects → the effect is in the shape (variance / tails / zeros), not the mean — the mean-only report would miss it;
    • \(t\) rejects but KS does not → a small mean shift on otherwise-identical shapes (rare here);
    • both silent → no detectable distributional change.
  4. Zero-inflation probe. Compute the share of zero-spend users in each arm. If they differ, explain why the right tool for “share of zeros” is a \(z\)-test for proportions (run it), and why an ARPU mean can stay flat while users churn underneath it.

3.5 The Independence Check (Research)

The two-sample KS — and the whole “two groups” framing — assumes the arms are independent. A classic A/B anti-pattern compares a metric before and after on the same users, which makes the samples dependent and the two-sample KS conservative and weak.

  1. Check it. Compare the user ids of the two arms: how many users appear in both exp.uid_control and exp.uid_test (np.intersect1d)? State whether your arms are independent (disjoint users) or paired (shared users).
  2. If independent: confirm in one sentence that the two-sample KS in Section 3.3–Section 3.4 was the right tool, and move on.
  3. If paired: the two-sample KS was the wrong method. Redo the analysis on the per-user differences \(d_i = \text{after}_i - \text{before}_i\) (i.e. exp.test - exp.control, since the rows are aligned by user):
    • run check_normality(d) (or on \(\log\) differences) and a one-sample test of \(d\) against \(0\);
    • show, side by side, that the naive two-sample KS missed the effect while the paired analysis finds it, and explain why (the shared user component glues the two CDFs together).
TipResearch hook

This is the part you must discover, not assume. Most ids are independent; some are not. Reporting “I checked the user ids and they are disjoint, so the two-sample test is valid” is a real finding — and skipping the check on a paired dataset is a real mistake.

3.6 Validate Your Guardrail with Monte Carlo

Your KS guardrail is itself a criterion, so validate it as in Practice 6 / Lab 5: does it keep the promised 5% false positives on skewed ARPU data, and from what \(n\)? Use arpu_sample as your “god” generator — you set the truth, so you always know the right answer.

  1. Implement ks_fpr(gen, n, n_exps=2000, alpha=0.05, seed=...) that runs an A/A (\(H_0\) true: both groups from the same gen) n_exps times and returns the FPR with a 95% Wilson CI (proportion_confint(method="wilson")).
  2. Calibration. Run it for arpu_sample(250, n) at a small, a medium, and a large \(n\) (e.g. 20 / 100 / 1000). Report each FPR with its CI and whether \(\alpha = 0.05\) is inside. Plot FPR (with the Wilson band) vs. \(n\) with a dashed line at \(0.05\). Explain the small-\(n\) behaviour you see (conservative? why?).
  3. Power for your effect. Implement ks_power(gen_c, gen_t, n, ...) and measure the power of the KS guardrail to detect an effect like the one in your experiment (a mean shift, a shape/variance change, or a zero-spike — whichever matches your diagnosis from Section 3.4). How large an \(n\) would you need for power \(\ge 0.8\)?
  4. Real-log A/A (optional but encouraged). Load load_my_log(STUDENT_ID), split each (city, cuisine) slice in half with an independent random permutation (no treatment → \(H_0\) true), run your same_distribution on each, and report the A/A FPR with a Wilson CI. Does it match your synthetic calibration?
ImportantMatch the validation to your diagnosis

The criterion you validate must be the one your verdict rests on:

  • Small / skewed sample (the “new city” case): your verdict may hinge on the \(t\)-test, not KS — so validate that. Run the A/A FPR of the \(t\)-test on raw vs. log ARPU at your \(n\) and your arm balance (scipy.stats.ttest_ind). On a small, unbalanced skewed design the raw test can be miscalibrated (FPR off \(\alpha\)) while the log-scale test is on target — that calibration gap, not just the QQ-plot, is what justifies working on the log scale. (Note: a balanced raw \(t\)-test is far more robust to skew — so check your own balance before concluding.)
  • Paired design (shared users): the two-sample KS is the wrong guardrail here, so do not validate it as if it were right — validate the paired criterion you actually used in Section 3.5 (a one-sample test on the differences \(d_i\)), and show it recovers power the naive two-sample KS throws away.
NoteWhy this matters

Whatever you conclude in Section 3.3–Section 3.4 rests on the criterion being trustworthy at your sample size. If your \(n\) is small and the guardrail is conservative there, a “pass” is weaker than it looks — say so. “Conservative” (FPR below \(\alpha\)) is safe but underpowered; “invalid” (FPR above \(\alpha\)) is not. Keep that distinction from Lab 5.

3.7 Final Forensic Verdict

Write a single-page memo (≤ 400 words) to a non-technical GreenBowl PM who has only seen the platform’s headline \(t\)-test line. It must contain:

  1. The headline \(t\)-test verdict (what the automated report claimed) and your one-line forensic verdict — one of: a real mean win/loss; an effect hidden in the shape; a broken randomization (don’t trust it); a churn / zero-inflation problem under a flat mean; a metric that must be read on the log scale; or a paired design analysed the wrong way.
  2. The evidence that forces it, quoted as numbers, not adjectives: the normality decision (raw vs. log), the pre-period A/A result, the \(t\)-vs-KS comparison with the ECDF, the independence check, and the share of zeros if relevant.
  3. A concrete recommendation: ship / don’t ship / fix the splitter / report the distribution (not just the mean) / re-analyse as paired / collect more data — whichever your diagnosis demands.
  4. One honest caveat or limitation of your own audit (e.g. KS is conservative at your \(n\); KS is sensitive to everything and does not tell you whether the difference is business-significant; simulation noise at the n_exps you used).

It must be readable by someone who has never heard the words “Kolmogorov” or “\(p\)-value”. You are graded on whether the PM could act on it without follow-up questions.

Defense

During the defense (5–7 minutes) you will be asked “What if?” questions that probe whether you understand why each piece exists, for example:

  • “Shapiro rejected normality on your data. Does that kill the \(t\)-test, or not? What single thing settles it — the \(p\)-value or something else?”
  • “Your \(t\)-test said ‘no effect’ but your KS rejected. Explain to the PM, in one sentence, what changed in the experiment.”
  • “Your pre-period A/A failed. Why does that make the experiment-period result meaningless, no matter how small the \(p\)-value there is?”
  • “Show me — live — how many users your two arms share. If they overlap, what must you do differently, and why does the two-sample KS mislead you here?”
  • “Your KS guardrail’s FPR came out at \(0.032\) with CI \((0.026, 0.040)\) at \(n=20\). Is it broken, conservative, or just noisy? Which, and how do you know?”
  • “Your ARPU mean is flat but 8% of the test group spent zero. Why is a \(z\)-test for proportions the right tool there, and not KS?”

You may also be asked to change one thing live (switch raw ↔︎ log, swap the pre-period for the experiment period, shrink \(n\) in the Monte-Carlo, reshuffle the A/A split) and explain the resulting shift.
