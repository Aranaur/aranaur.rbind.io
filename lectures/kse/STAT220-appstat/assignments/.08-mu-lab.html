1 Objective

In Lab 7 you learned that a tidy “chi-square is significant” line can hide six different realities. Lab 8 returns to a numeric metric — delivery time — and takes away the crutch you have leaned on since Lab 4: the mean. Product metrics like delivery minutes are heavily right-skewed with the odd stuck-in-traffic outlier, and the platform’s headline verdict — a Welch \(t\)-test on the means — is exactly the test that skew and outliers break.

The GreenBowl experimentation platform A/B-tested a new courier dispatcher. For every order it logged the delivery time in minutes (lower = better) and the courier who carried it. The automated report prints a Welch-\(t\) “ship / don’t ship” line. Your job is to decide whether you can believe it.

The lecture gave you a family of tests that read only the direction or the order of the data, plus the guardrails around them:

  • Sign test — keep only the direction of each change (binomial); also the one-sample test of a median against a target KPI.
  • Wilcoxon signed-rank — rank the sizes of paired changes; needs symmetric differences.
  • Mann–Whitney U — two independent groups compared by ranks; its \(H_0\) is \(F = G\), not “the medians are equal”.
  • Effect sizes — the probability of superiority \(\hat P(A<B) = U/NM\) and the Hodges–Lehmann shift in minutes, with a distribution-free CI.
  • Brunner–Munzel — the honest rank test when the two groups have different shapes (Practice 8’s “shape trap”).
  • Monte Carlo — the course-long habit: validate whatever criterion your verdict rests on, at your \(n\) and your kind of data.

The twist, as in Labs 4–7: you do not get to see what is wrong. Your experiment is pulled live from greenbowl_mu.py, keyed to your student id. Some students received a genuine, business-meaningful speed-up; some an honest null; some a mean “win” manufactured by a handful of outliers; some a microscopic shift made “significant” by an enormous sample; some a dispatcher that keeps the median but wrecks the tail; and some a crossover design where the same couriers appear in both arms — so every independent-samples test is invalid before it starts. The only honest way to find out which one you hold is to run the diagnostics.

You will:

  1. Pull your experiment and read the platform’s headline Welch-\(t\) verdict — the one you are about to put on trial.
  2. Run design & shape forensics: is the design really two independent groups? What does the metric’s distribution look like, and can the mean be trusted with it?
  3. Warm up on the SLA sign test — one sample against the contractual 45-minute median.
  4. Build the rank toolkit step by step — mw_report → ab_report → robust_ab — or, if your design turns out to be paired, the sign_test / signed_rank pair on within-courier differences.
  5. Check the shape trap: what Mann–Whitney’s \(H_0\) really claims, and when Brunner–Münzel (or plain quantiles) is the honest answer.
  6. Validate with Monte Carlo — the FPR and power of the exact criterion your verdict rests on, at your \(n\).
  7. Deliver a one-page forensic verdict a non-statistician PM can act on.

3.2 Design & Shape Forensics (design_check)

Before choosing any test, answer two questions the headline never asks. First: what is the design? Independent-samples tests (Welch \(t\), Mann–Whitney) require the two arms to be independent — no shared users, no repeated measures. If the same courier appears in both arms, you have a crossover and must switch to the paired family. Second: what is the shape? If the metric is heavily skewed, the mean answers a question nobody asked.

  1. Implement Version 1 of your toolkit, design_check(exp), that returns: whether any courier_id appears in both arms (→ paired!), the arm sizes, and per-arm skew diagnostics (mean vs median gap, P90/P50 ratio). Hint: exp.data["courier_id"].duplicated().any(), or compare the sets of ids per arm.
  2. Run it. State your design: independent A/B or paired crossover. This single boolean decides which half of the lecture applies to you — and whether the platform’s headline test was even legal.
  3. Plot the two arms’ distributions (overlaid histograms and an ECDF plot). Describe the shape in one sentence: skew, outliers, tails. Mark the means and medians on the histogram — how far apart are they, and why?
  4. In one sentence: given this shape, what could make a mean-based verdict lie, and which summaries would you trust instead?
TipResearch hook

Most students will find an honest independent design here — and that finding is worth one sentence, not zero: “I checked, no courier appears twice, the independent-samples family applies.” For a few of you this check will overturn the entire analysis. Skipping it and being wrong is the single most expensive mistake in this lab.

3.3 Warm-up: the SLA Sign Test (sign_test)

The courier contract promises a median delivery time ≤ 45 min in the control (current) experience. Before any A/B question, the platform must verify the SLA — and the right tool for “is the median above the target?” on a skewed metric with zero distributional assumptions is the one-sample sign test: count how many orders exceed the target and compare with a fair coin, \(T = \#\{x_i > m_0\} \sim \text{Binom}(n, \tfrac12)\) under \(H_0\).

  1. Implement sign_test_median(x, m0, alternative="greater", alpha=0.05) using scipy.stats.binomtest (drop exact ties with m0 first). Return the count above target, \(n\), the \(p\)-value, and a verdict.
  2. Run it on your control arm against SLA_MIN: test \(H_1\): “median > 45 min”. Report the sample median next to the verdict.
  3. Interpret for the contract owner in one sentence — and be careful about the direction of the logic: “not rejected” means consistent with the SLA, not proven to meet it (recall the lecture’s KPI example).

3.4 The Right Test for Your Design (mw_report → ab_report)

Now the headline question, answered with the machinery your design permits. Follow the branch your Section 3.2 verdict dictates — and in both branches, report an effect size in minutes, not just a \(p\)-value.

3.4.1 Branch A — independent A/B (most students)

The rank answer to “did the new dispatcher speed deliveries up?” is Mann–Whitney plus two effect sizes: the probability of superiority \(\hat P(\text{test} < \text{control})\) — the share of random order pairs where the test order arrives sooner — and the Hodges–Lehmann shift — the median of all pairwise differences, in minutes, with its distribution-free CI (the inversion of the U-test from the lecture).

  1. Implement Version 2, mw_report(control, test, alpha=0.05): run mannwhitneyu, return \(U\), the \(p\)-value, \(\hat P(\text{test} < \text{control})\), and a verdict. (Mind the direction: scipy reports \(U\) for its first argument; check which orientation of \(U/NM\) gives “test is faster” and say so in a comment.)
  2. Implement Version 3, ab_report(control, test, alpha=0.05, mde=BUSINESS_MDE), that reuses mw_report and adds the Hodges–Lehmann shift with its 95% CI (lecture code: np.subtract.outer → sorted pairwise differences → the rank-quantile indices). If an arm is longer than ~2 000 observations, compute HL on a random subsample of 2 000 per arm (the \(NM\) pairwise matrix does not fit in memory at 150k×150k) — and say you did.
  3. Run ab_report. Report \(p\), \(\hat P(\text{test}<\text{control})\), the HL shift in minutes with its CI, and — separately — the decision layer: is the shift, at its CI’s optimistic and pessimistic ends, past BUSINESS_MDE? “Statistically real” and “worth shipping” are two different sentences; write both.
  4. Put your rank verdict next to the headline Welch \(t\) from Section 3.1. If they agree, say what that adds. If they disagree, this is the heart of your lab: find why (re-plot; try the comparison with the most extreme 1–2% of values winsorised; look at where mean and median moved). Which verdict do you trust, and what exactly is the other one reacting to?

3.4.2 Branch B — paired crossover (if design_check found shared couriers)

The arms share couriers, so independent tests are void — including the platform’s headline. Collapse each courier to their difference \(d_i = \text{control}_i - \text{test}_i\) (positive = the new dispatcher made courier \(i\) faster) and test that, exactly as in Practice 8 Part I–II.

  1. Build the per-courier paired frame (exp.data.pivot(index="courier_id", columns="arm", values="minutes")) and compute the differences \(d_i\).
  2. Implement sign_test(before, after) and signed_rank(before, after) (reuse the Practice 8 versions), plus the paired \(t\) (ttest_rel) for contrast. Run all three, one-sided (“the new dispatcher is faster”).
  3. Check the symmetry of the \(d_i\) histogram before trusting signed-rank — if it is visibly asymmetric, the sign test is your primary verdict, and say so.
  4. Report the median per-courier saving in minutes and the share of couriers who improved. Decision layer: is the saving past BUSINESS_MDE? Be honest if it is statistically clear but sitting on the business threshold — that is a “ship, but re-measure at scale” recommendation, not a triumphant one.
  5. Explain in two sentences why the pooled Welch \(t\) (and a pooled Mann–Whitney) missed what the paired analysis sees — where did the between-courier variance go?
ImportantStop-the-line rule

If your design is paired and you report a pooled independent-samples \(p\)-value anyway, everything downstream is void — the observations are not independent, so the test’s null distribution is simply not the one it was calibrated on. The design check is not bureaucracy; it decides which mathematics exists for you.

3.5 The Shape Trap (robust_ab)

Mann–Whitney’s strict null is \(F = G\) — the whole distributions match. It reads cleanly as a median/shift test only when the two shapes are the same. If the new dispatcher changes the spread (some orders much earlier, some much later), then: (a) the means move even when medians do not (a lognormal’s mean grows with its variance) — fooling the headline \(t\); (b) \(P(A<B)\) can sit at exactly \(0.5\) — so Mann–Whitney sees nothing; and (c) with unequal group sizes, MW’s calibration itself breaks (Practice 8, Part IV). A “no change” from MW plus a “slower!” from Welch \(t\) can both be wrong about the thing the business cares about.

  1. Compare the two arms’ shape, not just location: the variance (or IQR) ratio, and a quantile table — P10 / P50 / P90 per arm. Overlay the two ECDFs. Do the curves shift or rotate (cross)?
  2. Run brunnermunzel(control, test) next to your mw_report. Recall from Practice 8: BM tests stochastic superiority while staying calibrated when shapes and variances differ (and MW’s own FPR can drift far from 5% with unequal \(n\) and unequal spreads — your Monte-Carlo section can prove this for your sizes).
  3. Implement Version 4, robust_ab(exp, alpha=0.05, mde=BUSINESS_MDE) — your all-in-one entry point, which reuses everything so far: design_check first (paired → route to the sign/signed-rank branch); otherwise compare shapes (variance/IQR ratio and the quantile table); if shapes clearly differ, report Brunner–Münzel + quantiles as the honest reading; else ab_report. Use robust_ab as your final analytical word.
  4. If your quantile table shows the tail moving while the median stands still — say it in business language: the median customer is unaffected, but the P90 customer waits X minutes instead of Y, and SLA breaches went from a% to b% (compute the share of orders above SLA_MIN per arm). Which headline — “ship”, “no change”, “roll back” — does that support, and is it any of the ones the tests printed?
TipResearch hook

One of the hidden worlds has equal medians and \(P(A<B) = 0.5\) exactly — a rank test cannot see it, by construction, and the mean test “sees” it for the wrong reason. If every test you ran feels weirdly contradictory, stop testing and look at the two ECDFs. The answer is a picture, not a \(p\)-value.

3.6 Validate Your Criterion with Monte Carlo

Whatever your verdict rests on — MW, BM, the sign test on differences — it is itself a criterion, and the course-long habit applies: check that it keeps its promised 5% false positives on data shaped like yours, at your sample sizes, and measure what it could have detected. Reuse the rate / prate engines from Practice 8 (rebuild them briefly in your report; they are your own code now).

  1. FPR (A/A). Simulate the null with a right-skewed law that mimics your control arm (rng.lognormal(np.log(median), sigma, n) with your arm’s median and a plausible sigma), at your \((n_{\text{control}}, n_{\text{test}})\), 2 000+ experiments, and report the FPR of your primary criterion with a Wilson CI. Is \(0.05\) inside?
  2. Stress the calibration the way your data stresses it. Pick the stress relevant to your experiment: unequal spreads at unequal \(n\) for MW vs. Brunner–Münzel (the Practice 8 Part-IV table, at your sizes); or an A/A with a few injected extreme outliers for Welch \(t\) vs. MW; or — if you are in the paired branch — asymmetric null differences for signed-rank vs. the sign test. Show the FPR table and say which criterion stays honest.
  3. Power / MDE. Inject a real effect of the kind your world suggests (a multiplicative median shift for location; your observed per-courier lift if paired) and estimate the power at your \(n\); then find (roughly) the smallest shift in minutes your setup detects with power ≥ 80%. If your verdict was “no effect”, this is the line that makes it informative: “we would have seen anything ≥ X min”.
ImportantMatch the validation to your diagnosis

A student in the outlier world should be validating robustness to outliers; a student with unequal arm sizes and different spreads should be validating MW’s FPR at those sizes; a student in the paired world should be validating the paired criteria on asymmetric differences; a student with a null should be reporting power, not just a passing FPR. A generic copy-pasted A/A that ignores what your data looks like earns the methodology point only halfway.

3.7 Final Forensic Verdict

Write a single-page memo (≤ 400 words) to a non-technical GreenBowl PM who has only seen the platform’s headline Welch-\(t\) line. It must contain:

  1. The headline verdict (what the automated report claimed) and your one-line forensic verdict — one of: a real, business-meaningful speed-up (ship); an honest null with a stated detectable-effect floor (don’t ship, and stop the experiment); a mean artifact from stuck-order outliers (don’t ship; fix the headline’s metric); a significant-but-trivial shift (don’t ship); a same-median, fatter-tail reliability regression (don’t ship; the tail, not the average, is the story); or a paired crossover the platform analysed with the wrong test (re-analyse paired; then decide — state your paired verdict).
  2. The evidence that forces it, quoted as numbers, not adjectives: the design check, the shape summary, the \(p\)-values you trust and the ones you overrule, \(\hat P(\text{test}<\text{control})\), the Hodges–Lehmann shift (or median per-courier saving) in minutes with its CI against the 3-minute business threshold, the quantile/SLA table where relevant, and the Monte-Carlo calibration/power at your \(n\).
  3. A concrete recommendation: ship / don’t ship / fix the metric in the headline report / re-run the analysis paired / collect more — whichever your diagnosis demands.
  4. One honest caveat of your own audit (e.g. MW answers “stochastically faster”, not “the medians differ”, unless shapes match; HL on a subsample is an approximation; a sign test throws away magnitude; simulation noise at your n_exps).

It must be readable by someone who has never heard the words “Mann–Whitney” or “\(p\)-value”. You are graded on whether the PM could act on it without follow-up questions.

Defense

During the defense (5–7 minutes) you will be asked “What if?” questions that probe whether you understand why each piece exists, for example:

  • “Your Welch \(t\) says the new dispatcher is faster and Mann–Whitney says nothing. Show me — live — the two or three orders that explain the disagreement, and tell me which verdict I should believe.”
  • “Your MW \(p\) is \(10^{-8}\) on 300 000 orders and the Hodges–Lehmann shift is \(-0.3\) minutes. Ship or not? What single number settles it?”
  • “The same courier id appears in both arms. What exactly breaks in mannwhitneyu — which assumption, and what happens to its null distribution?”
  • “Medians are equal, \(P(A<B) = 0.5\), yet you told the PM ‘don’t ship’. What did you see that the rank test is blind to, by construction?”
  • “Why did you trust the sign test over the signed-rank here? What did the histogram of differences show, and which extra assumption does signed-rank quietly add?”
  • “Your A/A says MW’s FPR is 11% at your group sizes. Which of your reported \(p\)-values does that invalidate, and what do you switch to?”

You may also be asked to change one thing live (winsorise the outliers, swap MW for Brunner–Münzel, pool the paired data and watch the effect vanish, shrink \(n\) in the Monte-Carlo) and explain the resulting shift.
