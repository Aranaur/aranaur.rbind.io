1 Objective

In Lab 1 you designed a one-sample test for your own business case (comparing the observed metric against a fixed historical baseline \(\mu_0\)).

In Lab 2 you went beyond accept/reject and built confidence intervals, ran an A/A on your one-sample infrastructure, and wrote a PM-readable memo.

Lab 3 takes the next, more realistic step: you switch from “observed vs. historical baseline” to a proper two-sample A/B test — two cohorts running in parallel, both with their own noise. The historical baseline becomes a sanity reference, not a parameter of the test.

You will:

  1. Convert your one-sample framing into a proper two-sample A/B design, stating the hypotheses in terms of \(p_1\) and \(p_2\).
  2. Wrap your test logic into a reusable my_z_test() function for the two-sample case, with the pooled-vs-unpooled SE distinction correctly implemented.
  3. Build the confidence interval for the lift and compare it with the reference implementation.
  4. Plan the experiment: compute power, MDE, and the required sample size, and contrast the answer with your one-sample plan from Lab 1.
  5. Validate the infrastructure with a two-sample A/A simulation, and (bonus) check for Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM).
  6. Write a stakeholder-ready recommendation memo.

3.2 From One Sample to Two: Design the A/B

In a real A/B experiment we do not compare against a frozen historical \(\mu_0\). We split incoming users into two cohorts — control (current product) and test (new variant) — and we test whether their true conversion rates differ.

  1. Define the two cohorts in product terms (who sees what, how users are assigned, what the randomisation unit is). Make it specific to your product.
  2. Restate the hypotheses in two-sample form: \[H_0: p_1 = p_2 \quad\text{vs.}\quad H_1: p_1 \, \square \, p_2,\] where \(\square\) is \(<\), \(>\), or \(\neq\). Choose the alternative and justify it in one sentence (e.g. “we only want to ship if the new variant is better, so one-sided ‘less’ for \(p_1 < p_2\)”).
  3. State the same \(\alpha\) and business MDE you used in Lab 2. The MDE is now the smallest interesting difference \(|p_2 - p_1|\), not a deviation from a fixed baseline.
  4. Briefly explain (one paragraph) why the historical \(\mu_0\) is no longer a parameter of the test. What role does it still play in planning the experiment?

3.3 A Reusable my_z_test() Function

  1. Implement a function my_z_test(x1, n1, x2, n2, alternative, alpha, conf_int) with the following contract:
    • Convention: \(Z = (\hat p_1 - \hat p_2) / SE_{\text{pool}}\); alternative is about \(p_1 - p_2\).
    • Supports alternative \(\in\) {"greater", "less", "two-sided"}.
    • Uses the pooled standard error \(SE_{\text{pool}} = \sqrt{\hat p_{\text{pool}}(1-\hat p_{\text{pool}})(1/n_1 + 1/n_2)}\) for the \(p\)-value.
    • When conf_int=True, also returns a CI for the lift \(p_1 - p_2\) built with the unpooled SE \(SE_{\text{unp}} = \sqrt{\hat p_1(1-\hat p_1)/n_1 + \hat p_2(1-\hat p_2)/n_2}\).
    • Returns a dict with at least: p1, p2, lift, z_stat, p_value, reject, ci_low, ci_high.
  2. Explain in one paragraph why the two SEs are different — when each one is the “right” SE, and what bug would result from using SE_pool for the CI (or SE_unp for the test).
  3. Apply the function to a small synthetic A/B based on your business case (you may invent realistic \(x_1, n_1, x_2, n_2\) at this point; the planned \(N\) comes in Section 3.5). Print the full result.
  4. Verify your implementation against statsmodels.stats.proportion.proportions_ztest — this is the only legitimate use of the built-in function. Show that the \(Z\)-statistic and \(p\)-value match to at least 4 decimal places. Be careful with the alternative mapping: statsmodels’s "smaller"/"larger" is in terms of prop1 - prop2.

3.4 Confidence Interval for the Lift

  1. For your synthetic A/B from Section 3.3, compute a \(95\%\) CI for the lift \(p_2 - p_1\) using three different constructions:
    • Wald (unpooled) via your own my_z_test.
    • The reference implementation confint_proportions_2indep(x2, n2, x1, n1, method="wald") (note the argument order: this function reports a CI for \(p_2 - p_1\)).
    • One better method via confint_proportions_2indep(method=...) (your choice: "newcomb", "agresti-caffo", or "score"). Briefly state, in one sentence, why this method exists — what does it fix relative to plain Wald?
  2. Put all three intervals into a table with columns method | lower | upper | width. Comment on:
    • Which interval is the narrowest, and why is that not a decisive reason to prefer it?
    • For which kinds of observed \((\hat p_1, \hat p_2)\) would the unpooled Wald interval cross zero in a way the better methods would not?
  3. Two business decisions read from the same CI. Translate the CI you computed into product language. Specifically, given the business MDE from Section 3.2:
    • If the CI for the lift is entirely above the MDE → ship.
    • If the CI straddles zero → no evidence of a difference.
    • If the CI is entirely above zero but its lower bound is below the MDE → there is a real lift, but it may not be worth shipping. State exactly what you would do in this case for your product.

3.5 Power and Sample-Size Planning

  1. Implement (or reuse from Practice 3) a function my_two_sample_z_power(p1, p2, n1, n2, alpha, alternative) based on the formula: \[ \text{Power}(\text{less}) = \Phi\!\left(\dfrac{-z_{1-\alpha} \cdot SE_{\text{null}} - (p_1 - p_2)}{SE_{\text{alt}}}\right), \] with the symmetric expression for "greater" and the two-tailed sum for "two-sided". \(SE_{\text{null}}\) uses \(\bar p = (p_1 + p_2)/2\); \(SE_{\text{alt}}\) uses the actual \(p_1, p_2\).
  2. Power vs. \(n\) curve. Plot power as a function of \(n_1 = n_2 = n\) for \(n\) from a small value up to a value where power is clearly above \(0.9\). Use \(p_1 = \mu_0\) (your baseline) and \(p_2 = \mu_0 + \text{business MDE}\). Mark on the plot:
    • The horizontal line at power \(= 0.8\).
    • The minimum \(n\) at which the curve crosses it.
  3. Plan the experiment. Compute the required \(N\) per group via bisection. Compare with your Lab 1/Lab 2 plan:
    • How many users do you need now per group?
    • How does that compare to your one-sample plan (which only needed one group of \(N_{\text{plan}}\))? Two-sample roughly doubles the total user requirement — but the math is more subtle than just “\(\times 2\)”. Explain in one paragraph why (two-sample N per group) is not simply equal to (one-sample N from Lab 1).
  4. MDE vs. \(n\) trade-off. Hold power at \(0.8\) and produce a table or plot of the MDE you can reliably detect as a function of \(n\) (per group). Pick three or four \(n\) values that are realistic for your product (e.g. one week / one month / one quarter of traffic). Translate each MDE into product language: “with one week of traffic we can only detect lifts of at least X pp, which means we are blind to anything smaller, which means …”

3.6 A/A Simulation (Two-Sample)

  1. Run \(\geq 5\,000\) simulated two-sample A/A experiments under \(H_0\) with your planned \(N\) per group and \(p_1 = p_2 = \mu_0\). For each one, compute the \(p\)-value using your my_z_test.
  2. Plot the histogram of \(p\)-values. Comment on its shape:
    • Why is it expected to be approximately uniform on \([0, 1]\)?
    • The two-sample Z-statistic is continuous, unlike the binomial — so the steps you saw in Lab 2 should be much smaller. Why?
  3. Report the empirical FPR (fraction of \(p \leq \alpha\)). Compute a \(95\%\) Wilson CI around the empirical FPR via proportion_confint(method="wilson"). Is the nominal \(\alpha\) inside it? Use this to argue, with simulation evidence, that the test is correctly calibrated.
  4. Two product-specific failure modes. Describe two realistic ways your A/A could fail for your specific product (not generic “the data are wrong”). Examples of the right level of specificity: “same-device cookie collisions between groups”, “treatment assignment leaks via cached static assets”, “weekend traffic is exclusively routed to group A”. For each failure mode, predict what the \(p\)-value histogram would look like and which direction the FPR would move.

3.7 Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM)

In real A/B platforms users are supposed to be split between control and test in a known ratio (most often 50/50, sometimes 90/10). A Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM) — where the observed split differs significantly from the planned one — is one of the strongest signals that something is broken in the assignment pipeline, and it invalidates the experiment before you even look at the lift.

  1. Implement an SRM check using \(\chi^2\) on a single \(1 \times 2\) table: \[ \chi^2 = \sum_{i=1,2} \dfrac{(n_i^{\text{obs}} - n_i^{\text{exp}})^2}{n_i^{\text{exp}}},\qquad \text{df} = 1. \] Compute the \(p\)-value via scipy.stats.chi2.sf(chi2, df=1).
  2. Apply your check to:
    • The synthetic A/B from Section 3.3 (it should pass if you set \(n_1 = n_2\)).
    • A clearly broken split (e.g. \(n_1 = 2100, n_2 = 1900\) when the plan was 50/50). Confirm that the check rejects.
  3. Run \(\geq 1\,000\) A/A simulations where each user is assigned to a group with probability \(0.5\). Plot the histogram of SRM \(p\)-values and confirm that the empirical FPR of the SRM check is close to \(\alpha\).
  4. One concrete failure scenario. Describe one realistic way your product could end up with SRM that has nothing to do with the randomiser (e.g. one variant is heavier and times out for slow connections, so those users never make it into the data → the broken group has fewer users). What would you do in production if the SRM check fails: roll back, debug, or both? In what order?

3.8 Final Recommendation

Putting all of the above together, write a single-page recommendation memo (≤ 400 words) addressed to a non-technical stakeholder. It must contain:

  1. The observed point estimates for control and test, the lift, and the \(95\%\) CI for the lift (state which construction and why).
  2. The decision (ship / iterate / extend the test) and the reason, framed in terms of the CI vs. the business MDE — not just the \(p\)-value.
  3. Two honest caveats:
    • Did the A/A check pass cleanly, or are there open questions about the infrastructure?
    • Did the SRM check pass? If you did not run SRM (bonus), say so and recommend running it before any production rollout.

The memo must be readable by someone who has never heard the words “\(p\)-value”, “pooled SE”, or “SRM”. You will be graded on whether a real product manager could act on it without follow-up questions.

TipGrading Details

Correctness of methodology (3 pts):

  • my_z_test implements all three alternatives correctly, uses pooled SE for the test and unpooled SE for the CI, and matches proportions_ztest to 4 decimals (1 pt).
  • The three CI constructions in Section 3.4 are computed correctly and the comparison table is consistent with the reference (0.5 pt).
  • The power calculation in Section 3.5 matches at least one independent reference (e.g. statsmodels.stats.proportion.power_proportions_2indep) to 2 decimals, and the planned \(N\) via bisection is the correct minimum (1 pt).
  • A/A simulation produces an empirical FPR whose Wilson CI contains the nominal \(\alpha\); the SRM check (if attempted) is correctly built and validated by its own A/A (0.5 pt).

Interpretation of results (3 pts):

  • The pooled-vs-unpooled paragraph is correct and self-contained — you can explain both which SE to use and why.
  • The “CI vs. business MDE” decision rule produces a concrete, product-specific recommendation, not a slogan.
  • A/A failure modes are realistic and product-specific (no generic “wrong data”).
  • The SRM scenario (if attempted) names a specific, plausible product failure rather than “the randomiser is bugged”.

Code quality and organization (2 pts):

  • my_z_test and my_two_sample_z_power are single reusable functions used across all subsequent sections (not re-implemented inline).
  • Plots have axis labels, titles, and legible legends.
  • Narrative text precedes every code block, and each section reads as a coherent argument rather than a dump of outputs.

Defense

During the defense (5–7 minutes) you will be asked “What if?” questions that probe whether you understand why each piece exists, for example:

  • “Your test uses pooled SE but your CI uses unpooled SE. Why is that not inconsistent? What would a single, unified SE buy or break?”
  • “The PM says: ‘I see a 95% CI of \([+0.001, +0.020]\) for the lift, and our business MDE is \(+0.010\). Just tell me yes or no.’ Give your one-sentence answer and your one-sentence rationale.”
  • “Your A/A FPR is \(0.062\) instead of \(0.050\). Is the difference statistically meaningful, or is it just simulation noise? Show me the Wilson CI on \(0.062\) and use it to answer.”
  • “Switch your test from one-sided ‘less’ to two-sided in your code right now. By how much does the lower CI bound for the lift move, and does that change your recommendation?”
  • “Set the test-group \(n\) to half of your planned \(N\) and re-run. The \(p\)-value is still below \(\alpha\), but the CI got wider. Which one drives your decision, and why?”
  • (Bonus) “Your SRM check returned \(p = 0.001\) on a 50/50 plan. Do you read the lift, debug the platform, or both? In what order, and why?”

You may also be asked to change one parameter live in your code (e.g. flip alternative, halve \(n_2\), double the MDE) and explain the resulting shift.
