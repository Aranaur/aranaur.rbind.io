1 Objective

In Lab 4 you were handed an A/B dataset and had to recover the right business decision. Lab 5 is one level up: you audit the tool that makes those decisions.

The GreenBowl data-engineering team shipped a new significance criterion, team_criterion, and wired it into the experimentation platform — it now decides “ship / don’t ship” on every A/B test in the company. Before you let it touch a single roll-out, your job is to validate it with Monte Carlo, exactly as the lecture taught: generate many experiments with a known answer, run the criterion on each, and count how often it is right.

The twist, as in Lab 4: you do not get to see what the criterion does. team_criterion is keyed to your student id. Some students received a perfectly correct test; some received one that is correct only under certain conditions; some received one that is quietly broken. The only honest way to find out which one you hold is to simulate.

You will:

  1. Confirm your tools on a known-good reference, then generalize simulate_rejections so it can validate any criterion (not just scipy’s ttest_ind).
  2. Measure your criterion’s false-positive rate (FPR) under \(H_0\) with a Wilson confidence interval — the calibration check.
  3. Find the minimum sample size from which it is trustworthy (if ever) — the “small new city” problem.
  4. Measure power under \(H_1\) and compare it head-to-head with the trusted Welch reference — is it valid and competitive?
  5. Run ≈1000 A/A tests on your historical log and reconcile the result with your synthetic findings.
  6. Deliver a one-page go / no-go verdict on the criterion to the platform team.

2 General Requirements

ImportantThe fixed context (same for everyone)
  • Metric: weekly ARPU per user, in UAH — continuous, positive, right-skewed (lognormal).
  • Baseline: \(\mu_0 = 250\) UAH.
  • Significance level: \(\alpha = 0.05\).
  • The criterion under audit: team_criterion from greenbowl_mc.py, keyed to your id.

ImportantEveryone’s criterion is different — on purpose

Your team_criterion is unique to your id. It might be the textbook Welch test, a variant that is valid only on large or balanced samples, or one that is anti-conservative at every sample size. There is no single verdict shared across the class — there is only the verdict your simulations support. Copying another student’s conclusion will be obviously wrong for your criterion.

3.1 Set Up and Sanity-Check Your Tools

Download greenbowl_mc.py into your working folder and load your criterion and historical log. Use one id consistently — your KSE email is recommended.

from greenbowl_mc import (load_my_criterion, load_my_log,
                          reference_welch, arpu_sample, BASELINE, ALPHA)
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
from statsmodels.stats.proportion import proportion_confint

STUDENT_ID = "your.email@kse.org.ua"   # <-- put YOUR id here
team_criterion = load_my_criterion(STUDENT_ID)
log = load_my_log(STUDENT_ID)

# both criteria share the same call signature:
#   crit(test, control, alternative="two-sided") -> p_value
demo_test    = arpu_sample(260, 200)
demo_control = arpu_sample(250, 200)
print("team   :", team_criterion(demo_test, demo_control, "greater"))
print("welch  :", reference_welch(demo_test, demo_control, "greater"))
print(f"baseline = {BASELINE}, alpha = {ALPHA}")
  1. State your STUDENT_ID. Confirm both team_criterion and reference_welch run and return a \(p\)-value on a sample you generate with arpu_sample.
  2. In one sentence, confirm you understand that what the criterion does is hidden, and your entire task is to characterise its behaviour by simulation.
  3. Do not form any opinion yet from this single call — one \(p\)-value tells you nothing about calibration. Say why.

3.2 A Universal simulate_rejections()

In Practice 5 your validator hard-coded scipy.stats.ttest_ind. To audit an arbitrary criterion you must pass the criterion in as an argument.

  1. Implement

    simulate_rejections(criterion, test_gen, control_gen, n_test,
                    n_control=None, n_exps=10_000, alpha=0.05,
                    alternative="two-sided", seed=...)

    that runs n_exps experiments, each time drawing test = test_gen(n_test) and control = control_gen(n_control), calling criterion(test, control, alternative), and counting rejections (\(p < \alpha\)). It must return the rejection rate and its 95% Wilson confidence interval (proportion_confint(..., method="wilson")).

    • When the two generators have equal means, the rate is the FPR.
    • When the test generator has a higher mean, the rate is the power.

  2. Sanity-check the validator on the reference. Run it with criterion=reference_welch, both generators arpu_sample(250, n), at a comfortable \(n\) (say 400). Confirm the FPR’s CI contains \(\alpha = 0.05\). This proves your loop is correct before you point it at the mystery criterion.

TipReproducibility

Seed your RNG so every run of the report is identical, but vary the seed at least once (Practice 5, “Your turn 1”) to see that the point estimate wobbles — that wobble is exactly why you always report the interval.

3.3 Is Your Criterion Calibrated? (FPR)

Now point the validator at team_criterion. Generate \(H_0\) yourself (two groups from the same ARPU distribution, equal means) and read off the FPR.

  1. Balanced, equal-variance, large \(n\). Run the FPR check for team_criterion with both groups arpu_sample(250, n, sigma=0.6) at a large \(n\) (e.g. 800). Report the FPR and its CI. Is \(\alpha = 0.05\) inside?
  2. Small \(n\). Repeat at a small “new city” size — sweep \(n \in \{5, 10, 20, 50\}\). Does the verdict change as \(n\) shrinks?
  3. Unbalanced and unequal-variance. Run the check with a noisy, smaller test arm and a tight, larger control arm — e.g. test = arpu_sample(250, n, sigma=0.8) with n_test=200 and control = arpu_sample(250, n, sigma=0.4) with n_control=800. (Means are still equal, so \(H_0\) holds.) Report the FPR and CI.
  4. Side-by-side. For each of the three settings above, run reference_welch on the same generators and tabulate setting | team FPR (CI) | welch FPR (CI). Where, if anywhere, does your criterion’s FPR depart from both \(\alpha\) and the reference?
NoteRead the three settings as a diagnosis, not three numbers

A criterion can be calibrated in one regime and broken in another. A bug that shows up only when the groups differ in size and spread points to a different fault than one that shows up only at small \(n\) — and a criterion whose FPR is off in every setting is broken outright. Name what your pattern implies.

3.4 From What Sample Size Can You Trust It?

If your criterion is miscalibrated at small \(n\) but recovers as \(n\) grows, there is a threshold worth knowing — the “this city is too small to conclude” line.

  1. Implement min_sample_size(criterion, test_gen, control_gen, grid, ...) that reuses simulate_rejections, sweeps the sample sizes in grid, and returns the smallest \(n\) at which \(\alpha\) first lands inside the FPR’s confidence interval (print each step, as in Practice 5).
  2. Run it for team_criterion on equal-mean generators across a sensible grid (e.g. range(5, 201, 5) for a small-\(n\) problem, or wider). Report the threshold — or state clearly that no \(n\) in your grid fixes it (some criteria never become calibrated; that is itself a finding).
  3. Plot the FPR (with its Wilson band) against \(n\), with a dashed line at \(\alpha = 0.05\). One figure should make your threshold (or its absence) obvious.

3.5 Power — and the Head-to-Head vs. Welch

A criterion that is valid is not automatically worth using: among valid criteria you want the most powerful one. Generate \(H_1\) (give the test group a real ARPU lift) and measure the true-positive rate.

  1. Using simulate_rejections with a higher test mean (e.g. arpu_sample(250 + lift, n) vs arpu_sample(250, n)) and alternative="greater", measure the power of team_criterion for a couple of (lift, \(n\)) settings — at least one realistic small lift (e.g. +10 UAH) and one clear one (e.g. +25 UAH).
  2. Head-to-head. On the same generators and settings, measure the power of reference_welch. Put both in a table with their CIs.
  3. Interpret with the FPR in mind:
    • If your criterion is valid (from Section 3.3–Section 3.4): is its power at least as high as Welch’s, or does it pay for something with lost power?
    • If your criterion is anti-conservative (FPR \(> \alpha\)): explain why its “higher” power is not a real advantage. (Hint: a criterion that rejects too often under \(H_0\) will also reject often under \(H_1\) — the power number is contaminated by the inflated FPR. Power may only be compared among valid criteria.)

3.6 ≈1000 A/A Tests on Your Historical Log

Synthetic data are only as good as your guess about the distribution. The lecture’s gold standard is to validate on real logs: slice them, and on each slice run an A/A (split users in half with no treatment, so \(H_0\) is guaranteed true).

def thousand_aa_tests(log, criterion, alpha=0.05, lift=0.0,
                      alternative="two-sided", seed=1):
    rng, rej, k = np.random.default_rng(seed), 0, 0
    for _, gdf in log.groupby(["city", "cuisine"]):
        arpu = gdf["arpu"].to_numpy()
        idx  = rng.permutation(len(arpu))      # INDEPENDENT split per slice
        half = len(arpu) // 2
        control = arpu[idx[:half]]
        test    = arpu[idx[half:2 * half]] * (1 + lift)   # lift=0 -> A/A
        rej += criterion(test, control, alternative) < alpha
        k   += 1
    lo, hi = proportion_confint(rej, k, alpha=0.05, method="wilson")
    return {"rate": rej / k, "ci": (lo, hi), "k": k}
  1. Run thousand_aa_tests(log, team_criterion) on your log. Report the number of slices, the A/A FPR, and its Wilson CI. Is \(\alpha\) inside?
  2. Reconcile with Section 3.3. The historical split here is balanced (50/50). If your synthetic check flagged a problem only in the unbalanced/unequal-variance or only in the small-\(n\) setting, does the balanced historical A/A reproduce it? Explain in one or two sentences why the synthetic stress test can catch a fault the historical A/A misses (and vice versa). This reconciliation is the heart of the section.
  3. Power on a simulated effect. There is no real effect in an A/A log, so simulate one with lift > 0 (e.g. +3%, +5%, +10%). Report power for team_criterion, and — if it is valid — compare with reference_welch.
  4. The independence trap. Replace the per-slice rng.permutation with the same fixed split reused on every slice (or sort by arpu before splitting) and re-run the A/A. Show what happens to the FPR and explain why a non-independent split breaks the validation regardless of how good the criterion is.

3.7 Final Verdict

Write a single-page memo (≤ 400 words) to the GreenBowl platform team who shipped the criterion. It must contain:

  1. A one-line verdict: APPROVE / APPROVE WITH CAVEAT (state the condition — e.g. “only for balanced designs”, “only for \(n \ge \dots\)”) / REJECT.
  2. The evidence that forces it: the FPR (with CI) in each regime you tested, the minimum-\(n\) threshold if any, and the power comparison vs. Welch — quoted as numbers, not adjectives.
  3. A concrete recommendation: should the platform adopt it, restrict it to certain experiments, or keep using Welch? If you would reject it, say what a correct criterion would have done differently.
  4. One honest caveat or limitation of your own audit (e.g. simulation noise at the \(n\) you used, reliance on a lognormal assumption, only 320 slices in the historical log).

It must be readable by an engineer who has not taken this course. You are graded on whether the platform team could act on it without follow-up questions.

TipGrading Details

Correctness of methodology (3 pts):

  • simulate_rejections takes the criterion as an argument, runs \(H_0\)/\(H_1\) correctly, and returns the rate with a Wilson CI; it is sanity-checked on reference_welch (1 pt).
  • The FPR is measured in all three regimes (balanced large-\(n\), small-\(n\), unbalanced unequal-variance) with correct CIs, and min_sample_size reuses the validator correctly (1 pt).
  • Power is measured under \(H_1\) and the ≈1000 A/A on the historical log uses an independent per-slice split with a correct CI (1 pt).

Interpretation of results (3 pts):

  • Section 3.3 reads the three regimes as a diagnosis (where, if anywhere, the FPR departs from \(\alpha\) and from Welch), not as three loose numbers.
  • The power section compares TPR only after establishing validity, and correctly explains why an anti-conservative criterion’s power is not a real advantage.
  • Section 3.6 reconciles the historical A/A with the synthetic findings (why a 50/50 balanced A/A can miss a fault the synthetic stress test catches), and the memo’s verdict matches the evidence.

Code quality and organization (2 pts):

  • simulate_rejections, min_sample_size, and thousand_aa_tests are single reusable functions used across all sections (the criterion is always passed in, never hard-coded).
  • Plots have axis labels, titles, a dashed \(\alpha\) line, and legible CIs.
  • Narrative precedes every code block; each section reads as an argument, not a dump of outputs.

Defense

During the defense (5–7 minutes) you will be asked “What if?” questions that probe whether you understand why each piece exists, for example:

  • “Your criterion’s FPR came out at \(0.058\) with CI \((0.053, 0.064)\). Is it miscalibrated, or is that simulation noise? What single change settles it?”
  • “You measured higher power than Welch. Convince me that is a feature and not a symptom. What must you check first?”
  • “Your synthetic check failed but the 1000 A/A passed (or vice versa). Which do you trust, and why can the two disagree?”
  • “Show me — live — what happens to your A/A FPR if I split each slice the same way every time instead of re-shuffling. Why?”
  • “From what \(n\) would you let the platform run this criterion in a small new city? Point to the number on your plot.”
  • “Halve n_exps right now. What happens to your confidence intervals, and could that flip one of your verdicts?”

You may also be asked to change one parameter live (flip alternative, shrink \(n\), change the lift, swap in reference_welch) and explain the resulting shift.
