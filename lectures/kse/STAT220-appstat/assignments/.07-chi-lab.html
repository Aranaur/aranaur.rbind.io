1 Objective

In Lab 6 you learned that a clean \(t\)-test verdict is not enough, because the \(t\)-test only ever looks at the mean. Lab 7 moves to the other half of product data — counts in categories — and the lesson sharpens: a single “the chi-square is significant, ship it” line is, more often than not, a trap.

The GreenBowl experimentation platform A/B-tested a new menu layout. For every order it logged the basket tier the user landed in — Economy / Standard / Large / Premium / VIP — and the city the order came from. The automated report prints a tidy chi-square line claiming the redesign changed the basket mix. Your job is to decide whether you can believe it.

The lecture gave you a toolkit where everything reduces to one idea — compare observed counts \(O\) with the counts \(E\) expected under a hypothesis, \(\tau = \sum (O-E)^2/E\) — and several guardrails that the headline number quietly skips:

  • Goodness-of-fit as an SRM check — was the randomization itself honest (did the arms arrive in the intended 50/50 split)?
  • The expected-count rule (\(E \ge 5\)) — is the table even dense enough for the chi-square approximation to be valid?
  • Chi-square homogeneity + standardized residuals — did the distribution move, and where?
  • Cramér’s V — on big \(n\) everything is “significant”; is the effect actually strong?
  • Yates / Fisher — on a tiny pilot, does the exact test agree with the approximation?
  • Test of independence — is an apparent win really driven by a confounding covariate (the city)?
  • Monte Carlo — is your own guardrail calibrated at your sample size?

The twist, as in Labs 4–6: you do not get to see what is wrong. Your experiment is pulled live from greenbowl_chi.py, keyed to your student id. Some students received a genuine, business-meaningful shift; some a difference that is statistically “significant” yet microscopic; some a broken split; some a micro-pilot too small for the chi-square approximation; some a rare category that quietly invalidates the test; and some an apparent win that is really a city confound. The only honest way to find out which one you hold is to run the diagnostics.

You will:

  1. Pull your experiment and read the platform’s headline chi-square verdict — the one you are about to put on trial.
  2. Run the SRM guardrail (a goodness-of-fit test of the delivered split against the intended 50/50) — the categorical A/A.
  3. Enforce the expected-count rule and build a safe_homogeneity tool that refuses to trust a sparse table.
  4. Run the homogeneity test, read the standardized residuals, and pair the \(p\)-value with Cramér’s V to separate “significant” from “strong”.
  5. Run the independence check (arm × city) and, if your covariate is imbalanced, stratify to expose a confound.
  6. Validate your guardrail with Monte Carlo — its FPR, the minimum \(n\), and its power for your kind of effect.
  7. Deliver a one-page forensic verdict a non-statistician PM can act on.

3.2 The SRM Guardrail: Was the Split Honest? (srm_check)

Before believing any effect, check that the experiment was allocated the way it was designed. A Sample-Ratio Mismatch (SRM) — the delivered arm sizes drifting from the intended 50/50 — is the categorical A/B world’s equivalent of a broken randomization: if the split is biased, who is in each arm is biased too, and every downstream comparison is contaminated. The right tool is a goodness-of-fit test of the two arm counts against the intended ratio.

  1. Implement Version 1 of your toolkit, srm_check(n_control, n_test, intended=INTENDED_SPLIT, alpha=0.05), using scipy.stats.chisquare with f_exp set to the intended split, returning the \(\chi^2\), the \(p\)-value, and a human-readable verdict. (This is a \(k=2\) goodness-of-fit test — recall the lecture’s “\(<10\) for a \(2\times1\) table” note on expected counts.)
  2. Run it on your arm sizes. State plainly: does the split pass or fail SRM, and by how many percentage points the realized ratio differs from 50/50.
  3. Interpret as a guardrail. A rejection here means the assignment / logging is broken — not that the feature did anything. Explain, in one sentence, why a difference you later see in the basket mix cannot be attributed to the layout if the SRM check fails.
ImportantStop-the-line rule

If the SRM check fails, the basket comparison is void until the allocation is fixed — a “win” on a biased split is most likely differential dropout (e.g. low-spenders silently missing from one arm), not a real effect. If it fails, say so plainly: the rest of your analysis becomes “here is the bug”, not “here is the effect”.

3.3 Expected-Count Hygiene (safe_homogeneity)

The chi-square test relies on a normal approximation (the CLT) that breaks for rare categories. The rule: every expected count \(E_{ij} \ge 5\) (and \(\ge 10\) for a \(2\times 2\)). A table can look fine — non-zero everywhere — yet have an expected count below 5 hiding in a rare column, and then the \(p\)-value is not to be trusted.

  1. Implement Version 2, homogeneity_test(table, alpha=0.05), using scipy.stats.chi2_contingency, returning \(\chi^2\), the degrees of freedom, the \(p\)-value, min_expected (the smallest expected count), and a verdict (mirror Practice 7’s assoc_test).
  2. Run homogeneity_test on your full \(2\times 5\) table and report min_expected. Is the rule satisfied? If min_expected < 5 (look at the rare VIP tier especially), merge VIP into Premium and re-run. Did the verdict change? (If your table is so small that chi2_contingency cannot even run — an all-zero column — that itself is a verdict: the table is too sparse for this test.)
  3. You will turn this guard into a reusable tool (safe_homogeneity) in Section 3.4, once the report version exists for it to fall back on.
WarningThe rule is about expected, not observed

A category can have a healthy observed count yet a tiny expected one, or vice versa. Always read min_expected from chi2_contingency, not the zeros you happen to see in the table.

3.4 Did the Mix Move, and Is It Strong? (homogeneity_report)

Now the headline question — but answered honestly. The chi-square homogeneity test says whether the basket distribution differs between arms; the standardized residuals say where; and Cramér’s V says how strongly — which the \(p\)-value, on a large sample, cannot.

  1. Implement Version 3, homogeneity_report(table, alpha=0.05), that reuses homogeneity_test (Version 2) and adds Cramér’s V, \(V = \sqrt{\chi^2 / (n\,(\min(m,k)-1))}\), plus a verbal strength label (practically none \(<0.1\), weak \(<0.2\), moderate \(<0.4\), else strong).
  2. Implement Version 4, safe_homogeneity(table, alpha=0.05), your all-in-one entry point, that reuses homogeneity_report and adds a guard: if the table is \(2\times 2\) and min_expected < 10 (the stricter \(2\times 2\) threshold from the lecture), switch to fisher_exact; otherwise, if any min_expected < 5, refuse to trust the raw chi-square and flag that a category must be merged (mirror Practice 7’s safe_assoc). Use safe_homogeneity from here on.
  3. Run it on the table you judged valid in Section 3.3 (raw or merged). Report \(p\), \(V\), and the strength label.
  4. If your full table was too sparse for a valid 5-tier test (every \(E < 5\), or chi2_contingency would not even run on an all-zero column — the micro-pilot case), do not report a 5-tier verdict. Collapse to the business-critical \(2\times 2\) — Premium+VIP (“upmarket”) vs the rest — and run safe_homogeneity on it: with small expected counts it will route to Fisher’s exact test. Report the exact \(p\) and treat that as your verdict for this experiment.
  5. Plot the standardized residuals \((O-E)/\sqrt{E}\) as a heatmap (imshow, diverging colormap, annotated). Read it in business terms: which basket tiers gained or lost in the test arm? Is the movement upward (toward Premium/VIP) or not?
  6. Significant ≠ strong. State explicitly: if your \(n\) is very large and \(p \approx 0\) but \(V\) is tiny, the change is real but trivial — quote the actual shift in the Premium+Large+VIP share in percentage points and judge whether it clears a business threshold. Conversely, a non-trivial \(V\) with an upward residual pattern is a genuine candidate to ship.
WarningCramér’s V is biased upward on small samples

\(V\) is inflated when \(n\) is small. Under \(H_0\), \(\mathbb{E}[V^2] = \dfrac{(m-1)(k-1)}{n\,(\min(m,k)-1)}\), so \[ \mathbb{E}[V] \lesssim \sqrt{\frac{(m-1)(k-1)}{n\,(\min(m,k)-1)}} \;\xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}\; 0 . \] The bias shrinks as \(n\) grows — that is the whole point. For a \(2\times 5\) table at \(n=30\) this floor is \(\sqrt{4/30}\approx 0.37\), which is exactly why a 15-vs-15 pilot shows \(V\approx 0.3\) with no real effect. Do not read that as a “moderate” association: a large \(V\) only means something at a healthy \(n\); at tiny \(n\), trust the exact-test \(p\)-value, not \(V\). (For an honest number, use the bias-corrected Cramér’s V, e.g. Bergsma’s.)

TipResearch hook

Two of the hidden worlds both reject the homogeneity test — but one is a real win and the other is a microscopic difference inflated by an enormous sample. The chi-square \(p\)-value cannot tell them apart. Cramér’s V and the percentage-point shift can. This is exactly the big-\(n\) trap from the lecture.

3.5 The Confound Check: Is the “Win” Really the City?

Homogeneity assumes the arms differ only by the treatment. But if the split accidentally over-sampled one city in the test arm — and that city has different basket tastes — the pooled table can show a “shift” that is pure covariate imbalance, not the layout. This is a test of independence question, and the fix is to stratify.

  1. Check the covariate balance. Build the arm × city table (exp.table(by="arm_city")) and run homogeneity_test on it. Is the city distribution independent of the arm, or did the test arm over-/under-sample some city?
  2. If arm × city is balanced: confirm in one sentence that the city is not confounding your basket comparison, and your Section 3.4 verdict stands.
  3. If arm × city is imbalanced: the pooled basket result is suspect. Stratify — run homogeneity_test on the basket table within each city (exp.table(by="city")) and compare. If the within-city tables stop rejecting while the pooled one rejected, the apparent effect is a city confound (a Simpson-style reversal): report the layout as not the cause, and recommend fixing the split / controlling for city. Read the four within-city \(p\)-values with multiplicity in mind (a single \(p\) just under \(0.05\) across four cities is expected by chance — think Bonferroni).
TipResearch hook

This is the part you must discover, not assume. Most ids have a balanced arm × city; some do not. “I checked arm × city, it is independent, so city is not confounding the result” is a real finding — and skipping the check when the covariate is imbalanced is a real mistake that ships a non-effect.

3.6 Validate Your Guardrail with Monte Carlo

Your homogeneity test is itself a criterion, so validate it as in Practice 7 / Lab 5: does it keep the promised 5% false positives at your table shape and sample size, and from what \(n\) can it detect an effect like yours? You set the truth, so you always know the right answer.

  1. Implement homogeneity_fpr(probs, n_per_arm, n_exps=2000, alpha=0.05, seed=...) that runs an A/A (\(H_0\) true: both arms drawn from the same probs with np.random.default_rng(...).multinomial) and returns the FPR with a 95% Wilson CI (proportion_confint(method="wilson")).
  2. Calibration. Run it for the GreenBowl base mix at a small, a medium and a large \(n\) (e.g. 30 / 200 / 2000). Report each FPR with its CI and whether \(\alpha = 0.05\) is inside. Then make a category rare (push VIP toward \(\approx 0.01\)) at small \(n\) and show the FPR leave \(\alpha\) — the Monte-Carlo proof of the \(E \ge 5\) rule from Section 3.3. Plot FPR (with the Wilson band) vs. \(n\) with a dashed line at \(0.05\).
  3. Power for your effect. Implement homogeneity_power(p_control, p_test, n_per_arm, ...) and measure the power to detect an effect like the one in your experiment (the basket shift, or — if your world is the trivial / null one — a deliberately small shift, to show how much \(n\) a tiny effect needs). How large an \(n\) per arm would you need for power \(\ge 0.8\)?
ImportantMatch the validation to your diagnosis

Validate the criterion your verdict actually rests on:

  • Micro-pilot (the 15-vs-15 case): your decision hinges on a small \(2\times 2\), so validate that — run the A/A FPR of the \(2\times 2\) chi-square with and without Yates’ correction at small \(n\) (reuse the Practice-7 chi2_rejection_rate idea), and show that without correction it over-rejects while Fisher / Yates holds \(\alpha\). That over-rejection is why you trusted Fisher over the naive chi-square.
  • Rare-category world: validate the \(E \ge 5\) rule directly (step 2) and show that merging restores an honest FPR.
  • Big-\(n\) trap (trivial world): the criterion is calibrated fine — the point is power, not FPR. Show that at your \(n\) even a microscopic effect is detected with power \(\approx 1\), which is exactly why \(p\) is uninformative about strength.
NoteWhy this matters

Whatever you conclude rests on the criterion being trustworthy at your table shape and size. “Conservative” (FPR below \(\alpha\)) is safe but underpowered; “invalid” (FPR above \(\alpha\), e.g. from a rare cell) is not — a rejection from an invalid test is worth nothing. Keep that distinction from Lab 5.

3.7 Final Forensic Verdict

Write a single-page memo (≤ 400 words) to a non-technical GreenBowl PM who has only seen the platform’s headline chi-square line. It must contain:

  1. The headline verdict (what the automated report claimed) and your one-line forensic verdict — one of: a real, business-meaningful basket shift (ship); a significant-but-trivial change (don’t ship); a broken split / SRM (fix the splitter, result void); an underpowered micro-pilot (collect more); a table invalidated by a rare category (fix the test, no effect); or an apparent win that is a city confound (don’t trust it, control for city).
  2. The evidence that forces it, quoted as numbers, not adjectives: the SRM check, min_expected and any merge, the homogeneity \(p\) and Cramér’s V, the residual pattern, the arm × city independence check (with stratified results if relevant), and the Monte-Carlo calibration/power at your \(n\).
  3. A concrete recommendation: ship / don’t ship / fix the splitter / merge and re-test / collect more / control for city — whichever your diagnosis demands.
  4. One honest caveat of your own audit (e.g. chi-square tells you whether, not whether it is business-significant; a single stratified \(p\) near \(0.05\) across four cities is multiplicity, not signal; simulation noise at the n_exps you used).

It must be readable by someone who has never heard the words “chi-square” or “\(p\)-value”. You are graded on whether the PM could act on it without follow-up questions.
