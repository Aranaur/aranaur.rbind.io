1 Objective

In Lab 3 you ran a proper two-sample A/B test on a proportion with the \(Z\)-test, where the variance was “self-describing” (\(\sigma^2 = p(1-p)\)).

Lab 4 moves to a continuous metric — ARPU (average revenue per user per week, in UAH) — where the variance is unknown and must be estimated from the data as \(S^2\). That single change (\(\sigma^2 \to S^2\)) is what turns a \(Z\)-test into a \(t\)-test, with all the consequences you saw in the lecture and in Practice 4: Student’s distribution on small samples, Welch vs. pooled, the CLT + Slutsky rescue on large samples, the MDE.

The twist: you do not get to invent your numbers. You will pull a live A/B dataset from the GreenBowl simulation platform, keyed to your own student id. The true data-generating parameters — the effect size, the two variances, the group sizes, even whether there is any effect at all — are hidden from you. Your job is to recover the correct business decision from the data alone, exactly as a real product analyst must.

ImportantEveryone’s data is different — on purpose

Your dataset is unique to your id. It might contain a large effect, a tiny one, or none. The variances of the two groups might be equal or wildly different. The sample might be plenty or far too small for the conclusions you want to draw. There is no single “right answer” shared across the class — there is only the answer your data supports. Copying another student’s conclusions will be obviously wrong for your dataset.

You will:

  1. Pull your A/B dataset and diagnose it (shape, variance, sample size).
  2. Wrap your test logic into a reusable my_two_sample_t_test() (Welch + pooled), verified against scipy.
  3. Choose Welch vs. pooled from your diagnostics, run the test, and build a confidence interval for the lift.
  4. Plan: compute power and the MDE you could realistically detect at your own sample size — and decide whether your experiment was even adequately powered.
  5. Validate the infrastructure with a two-sample A/A tuned to your variance and sample size, and stress-test it if your data are skewed.
  6. Write a stakeholder-ready recommendation memo.

2 General Requirements

ImportantThe fixed business context (same for everyone)
  • Metric: weekly ARPU per user, in UAH — continuous, positive, right-skewed.
  • Baseline: \(\mu_0 = 250\) UAH (3 months of history for the old menu).
  • Cohorts: control = old menu, test = new menu (combo deals).
  • Break-even = +15 UAH — the decision threshold. Below this lift the extra revenue does not cover rebuilding the menu and logistics, so the feature is not worth shipping.
  • Design-MDE = +25 UAH — the planning threshold. This is the “clear win” the team sizes the experiment to detect (power \(0.8\)). It is a different number from break-even and is used only for power/sample planning, never as the ship rule.
  • Direction: we only ship if the new menu is better → one-sided test, \(H_1: \mu_{\text{test}} > \mu_{\text{control}}\).
  • Significance level: \(\alpha = 0.05\).
NoteTwo thresholds, two jobs — do not collapse them into one

Planning and deciding answer different questions and cost differently, so they get different numbers:

  • break-even (+15) answers “is the effect worth shipping?” → the ship decision is made relative to it.
  • design-MDE (+25) answers “which effect did we size the experiment to catch?” → it feeds the power/\(N\) calculation only.

The classic mistake is to require the CI lower bound to clear the design-MDE. That demands proving a +25 effect, while the experiment was only built to prove “\(> 0\)” — so it rejects genuinely profitable features (lift between +15 and +25). Decide relative to break-even; use the design-MDE only to judge whether your \(n\) was adequate.

Download greenbowl_ab.py (provided with the lab) into your working folder and load your A/B experiment. Use one id consistently — your KSE email is recommended.

from greenbowl_ab import load_my_ab, BASELINE, BREAKEVEN, DESIGN_MDE, ALPHA

STUDENT_ID = "your.email@kse.org.ua"   # <-- put YOUR id here

print(f"test    (new menu): n = {len(test)}")

      f"design-MDE = +{DESIGN_MDE} UAH,  alpha = {ALPHA}")
  1. State your STUDENT_ID and report \(n_{\text{control}}\), \(n_{\text{test}}\), the two sample means, and the observed lift \(\overline{\text{test}} - \overline{\text{control}}\).
  2. Confirm in one sentence that you understand the true parameters are hidden and that your entire analysis is an estimation problem.

3.2 Diagnose Your Dataset

Before choosing any test, look at what you were handed. This section drives every methodological choice you make later — do not skip it.

  1. Plot both groups: overlaid histograms (or KDEs) of control and test on the same axes, with vertical lines at the baseline \(\mu_0 = 250\) and at each sample mean.
  2. Report a small diagnostic table with, for each group: \(n\), mean, \(S\) (sample SD, ddof=1), and the sample skewness (scipy.stats.skew).
  3. Answer, with evidence from your numbers/plots, the three questions that decide your method:
    • Normality / skew: how skewed is each group? Is it close to normal, or heavy-tailed?
    • Equal variances? Compute the ratio \(S^2_{\text{test}}/S^2_{\text{control}}\). Is it near 1, or far from it?
    • Sample size vs. the CLT: given the skew you observe, is your \(n\) large enough for the \(t\)-statistic to be approximately normal (recall Practice 4: small \(n\) + skew breaks the test)?
  4. In one sentence each, write down the consequence of each answer for the rest of the lab (e.g. “test SD is 3× control SD and the groups are unbalanced → I must use Welch, not pooled”).

3.3 A Reusable my_two_sample_t_test()

  1. Implement my_two_sample_t_test(a, b, alternative, equal_var, alpha, conf_int) with this contract (mirror Practice 4):
    • Convention: \(T = (\bar a - \bar b)/SE\); alternative is about \(a - b\). Pass a = test, b = control so "greater" means test beats control.
    • Supports alternative \(\in\) {"greater", "less", "two-sided"}.
    • equal_var=False → Welch SE and Welch–Satterthwaite \(df\) (the default); equal_var=True → pooled SE with \(df = n_a+n_b-2\).
    • When conf_int=True, returns a dict with at least: diff, t_stat, df, p_value, ci_low, ci_high. The CI must match the alternative (two-sided → two bounds; one-sided → one finite bound and \(\pm\infty\)).
  2. Verify your implementation against scipy.stats.ttest_ind(test, control, alternative=..., equal_var=...) for both equal_var=True and equal_var=False. Show that the \(t\)-statistic and \(p\)-value match to at least 4 decimal places. This is the only legitimate use of the built-in function.

3.4 Welch vs. Pooled, Test, and CI for the Lift

  1. Choose equal_var based on Section 3.2, and justify it in one sentence using your variance ratio and group sizes.
  2. Run my_two_sample_t_test(test, control, alternative="greater", equal_var=<your choice>, conf_int=True). Report the lift, \(t\), \(df\), \(p\)-value, and the one-sided 95% lower bound for the lift.
  3. Show what the wrong choice would have done. Run the other equal_var setting too and put both in a table (method | df | p_value | ci_low). In one paragraph, explain which direction the pooled SE is biased for your data and why — and whether the choice changes your conclusion.
  4. Two-sided sanity line. Report the two-sided \(p\)-value as well and confirm \(p_{\text{two}} = 2\cdot\min(p_{\text{greater}}, p_{\text{less}})\) for your data.

3.5 Power and Sample-Size Planning

  1. Implement my_two_sample_t_power(mean_diff, sd, n, alpha, alternative) (normal approximation, equal \(n\), \(SE_{\text{diff}} \approx S\sqrt{2/n}\), as in Practice 4) and a plan_t_experiment(sd, mde, ...) that returns the \(N\) per group needed for power \(0.8\) (bisection or the closed form).
  2. Using \(S\) estimated from your pooled data, judge adequacy against the design-MDE = +25 UAH (the planning threshold, not the break-even):
    • Was your experiment adequately powered? Compute the power your actual sample size had to detect the design-MDE = +25 UAH.
    • Plan it properly. How many users per group would you have needed for power \(0.8\) at the design-MDE? Compare with the \(n\) you actually got.
  3. The decision layer (the heart of the lab). The ship decision is made relative to the break-even = +15 UAH, not the design-MDE. First quantify how profitable your effect is, treating it as \(\text{effect}\mid\text{data} \sim \mathcal N(\hat\Delta, SE)\):
    • report \(P(\text{effect} \geq \text{break-even}) = \Phi\!\big((\hat\Delta - 15)/SE\big)\) and the expected net value \(\hat\Delta - 15\) UAH/user.
    Then classify your result into exactly one of:
    • the lower bound clears the break-even (+15) → confidently profitable → a real, shippable win;
    • significant (\(p<\alpha\)) but the lower bound is below break-even → a real positive effect whose business value is uncertain → lean on \(P(\text{profitable})\) and expected value before shipping;
    • not significant, and well powered for the design-MDE (+25) → genuine evidence of no clear win → do not ship;
    • not significant and underpowered for the design-MDE → you simply do not have enough data to conclude — extend the test (state the \(N\) needed).
    State which case you are in and the evidence that rules out the others. (This is the crux: a non-significant \(p\)-value means very different things in the third vs. the fourth case; and being below the design-MDE is not a reason to kill a feature that clears break-even.)

3.6 A/A Validation (Tuned to Your Data)

  1. Run \(\geq 5\,000\) two-sample A/A simulations under \(H_0\), generating both groups from a lognormal with your estimated mean \(S\) and your group sizes (use np.random.lognormal; pick \(\mu,\sigma\) so the mean matches \(\mu_0\) and the SD matches your \(S\)). For each, compute the \(p\)-value with your my_two_sample_t_test.
  2. Plot the histogram of the \(p\)-values and report the empirical FPR (fraction \(\le \alpha\)) with a 95% Wilson CI (proportion_confint(method="wilson")). Is \(\alpha = 0.05\) inside it?
  3. Stress test for your situation. If Section 3.2 showed your data are heavily skewed and your \(n\) is small, the \(t\)-test may not be calibrated. Re-run the A/A at your actual (small) \(n\) and at a large \(n\) and compare the FPRs. Does the CLT rescue you at your sample size, or not? If your \(n\) is large, instead briefly explain why you expect the FPR to be on target regardless of skew (CLT + Slutsky).
  4. One product-specific failure mode. Describe one realistic way an A/A could fail for GreenBowl specifically (not generic “wrong data”) — e.g. “whales who order for a whole office are hashed into the same cohort by phone number”. Predict what the \(p\)-value histogram and the FPR would look like.

3.7 Final Recommendation

Write a single-page memo (≤ 400 words) to a non-technical GreenBowl PM. It must contain:

  1. Observed ARPU for control and test, the lift, and the 95% CI for the lift (state Welch or pooled, and why).
  2. The decision — ship / iterate / extend the test — and the reason, framed as the CI vs. the +15 UAH break-even (and, in plain words, how likely the lift is worth it), not just the \(p\)-value. Do not judge the feature against the +25 design-MDE — that number was only for sizing the experiment.
  3. Two honest caveats grounded in your own analysis, e.g. whether the A/A passed, whether the experiment was underpowered for the design-MDE, or whether skew on a small sample makes the \(t\)-test untrustworthy.

It must be readable by someone who has never heard the words “\(p\)-value” or “Welch”. You are graded on whether a real PM could act on it without follow-up questions.

Interpretation of results (3 pts):

  • Section 3.5 places the result in the correct one of the four cases (decision vs. break-even, adequacy vs. design-MDE) and rules out the others with evidence, including \(P(\text{effect} \geq \text{break-even})\) — this is the key deliverable.

Defense

During the defense (5–7 minutes) you will be asked “What if?” questions that probe whether you understand why each piece exists, for example:

  • “Your \(p\)-value is above \(0.05\). Convince me this means ‘no effect’ and not ‘not enough data’. What number settles it?”
  • “You chose Welch. Show me — live — what the pooled \(p\)-value would have been on your data, and explain which way it is biased and why.”
  • “Your 95% lower bound for the lift is, say, \(+6\) UAH and the break-even is \(+15\). The PM says ‘but \(p < 0.05\), ship it’. Give your one-sentence answer.”
  • “Why do we size the experiment for the +25 design-MDE but decide at the +15 break-even? What goes wrong if you instead require the lower bound to clear +25?”
  • “Your data are skewed and \(n\) is small. Your A/A FPR came out at, say, \(0.071\). Is the \(t\)-test miscalibrated here, or is that simulation noise? Show me the Wilson CI and decide.”
  • “Switch your alternative to two-sided right now. By how much does the lower CI bound move, and does your recommendation change?”

You may also be asked to change one parameter live (flip alternative, toggle equal_var, halve \(n\), double the MDE) and explain the resulting shift.
